### **Poll 1: Strategy & Priority**
**Question:** If your primary goal is to improve application resilience, where should you start?
*   A) Implement a multi-region active-active setup
*   B) Maximize single-region resilience first
*   C) Build a cross-region backup/restore process
*   D) Move all data to a global database

**Correct Answer/Insight:** **B) Maximize single-region resilience first.**
**Why?** The sources emphasize that multi-region is not a "magic button" and you can do a significant amount of work to improve resiliency within a single region before taking on the complexity of multiple regions. Multi-region is an extreme use case that should be driven by specific business requirements, such as irreversible brand damage or massive revenue loss.

### **Quiz 1: Data & The CAP Theorem**
**Question:** When using **synchronous replication** across regions to ensure data consistency, what is the primary trade-off?
*   A) Risk of significant data loss during failover
*   B) Increased application latency and potential "shared fate"
*   C) Simplified deployment pipelines
*   D) Lower infrastructure costs

**Correct Answer:** **B) Increased application latency and potential "shared fate."**
**Insight:** Synchronous replication requires a transaction to be committed in both the primary and standby regions before acknowledging it to the client. This introduces "orders of magnitude" more latency and creates a **shared fate**—if replication is broken, the application may become unavailable to the client because it cannot complete the write to both locations.

### **Poll 2: Operational Readiness**
**Question:** True or False: "An untested Disaster Recovery (DR) strategy is not a DR strategy."
*   A) True
*   B) False

**Correct Answer/Insight:** **A) True.**
**Why?** According to the sources, organizations must move beyond theoretical plans and **actively test** their failover implementations. Some companies, like Vanguard, use a "Follow the Sun" model that effectively tests their regional switching **three times a day** as part of standard operations, ensuring they can be confident the system works when needed.

### **Quiz 2: Architectural Patterns**
**Question:** In a **"Pilot Light"** multi-region pattern, what is the state of the secondary region?
*   A) It has identical capacity and is serving 50% of traffic
*   B) It has zero data but all compute resources are running
*   C) It has data, configuration, and code available, but compute resources are only scaled up during failover
*   D) It is only used for read-only traffic

**Correct Answer:** **C) It has data, configuration, and code available, but compute resources are only scaled up during failover.**
**Insight:** This is a cost-effective middle ground between "Backup and Restore" and "Active-Hot Standby". You keep the "core" of the application ready (the pilot light), but you don't pay for full compute capacity until it is actually needed.

---

### **Poll 3: Dependency Management**
**Question:** What is the biggest risk of having an application in Region B depend on a service endpoint located only in Region A?
*   A) Lower infrastructure costs
*   B) Improved data consistency
*   C) Creating a "Shared Fate" that defeats the purpose of multi-region
*   D) Faster deployment cycles

**Correct Answer/Insight:** **C) Creating a "Shared Fate" that defeats the purpose of multi-region.**
**Why?** The goal of multi-region architecture is to create **isolated stacks**. If your secondary region depends on the primary region to function (for authentication, third-party endpoints, or failover triggers), a failure in the primary region will take down the secondary region as well.

---

**Analogy for your LinkedIn post:**
Building a multi-region architecture without first maximizing single-region resilience is like **buying a second car because you’re afraid the first one will break down, but never changing the oil in either.** It’s better to maintain and reinforce your primary vehicle before doubling your costs and maintenance effort.
